## üß† What I Learned / Key Takeaways
- **MAC addresses are tied to the NIC** - if you remove the NIC, the MAC address goes with it. Makes sense now why they're called "physical addresses"
- **ARP is the bridge between Layer 2 and Layer 3** - maps IP addresses (logical) to MAC addresses (physical) so switches can actually deliver frames
- **DHCP uses 4-step process (DORA)**: Discover, Offer, Request, Acknowledge. Different from TCP's SYN-ACK handshake - they serve different purposes
- **OSI model is a troubleshooting framework** - not just theory. Can work bottom-up (physical to application) or top-down depending on the problem
- **Subnetting is about efficiency and security** - isolating networks (like employee vs guest WiFi) prevents security issues and reduces broadcast traffic
- Private IPs (192.168.x.x, 10.x.x.x) are for internal networks, public IPs for Internet access. Multiple devices share one public IP through NAT
- **Layer 3 devices (routers) strip and replace MAC addresses at each hop** but IP addresses stay the same end-to-end - this clicked for me
- IPv6 has 340 trillion trillion trillion addresses (2^128) vs IPv4's 4.3 billion - finally understand why we needed it
- Switches learn MAC addresses dynamically and only forward frames to the correct port (vs hubs flooding everything)
