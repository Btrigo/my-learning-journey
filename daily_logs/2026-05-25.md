**Jimmy Tries World — Home Networking Overview:**
- Core concept: home networking = managing how devices communicate within your LAN — most people's "network" is just Wi-Fi through a consumer router
- **Modem vs Router**: modem translates the ISP signal; router handles Wi-Fi and wired device connections — many ISP-provided devices (like AT&T fiber) combine both
- **Unmanaged vs Managed Switches**: unmanaged = plug-and-play, no control (cheap, fine for basic use); managed = VLAN support, STP loop prevention, port speed control, activity monitoring — much more relevant for homelab/IT work
- **VLANs**: segment traffic on one physical switch — e.g. separate VLAN for smart home, IoT, guests, work devices — reduces attack surface and improves performance
- **Running Ethernet**: patch panels centralize all drops; for rentals/older homes — options include MoCA adapters (ethernet over coax), powerline adapters, or just surface-running cables
- **PoE (Power over Ethernet)**: powers devices like access points, cameras, and small switches over the ethernet cable itself — eliminates need for a separate power outlet; flavors are PoE, PoE+, and PoE++ (more + = more watts)
- **Cable recommendations**: Cat6 or Cat6A for residential — supports up to 10 Gbps; most consumer devices cap at 1 Gbps but 2.5G/10G switches matter for NAS, video editing, media servers
- **Switch topology**: don't loop switches — use a branching/tree structure; chaining is fine up to ~3-4 deep, beyond that can cause instability
- **Access points vs mesh**: wired APs (like Ubiquiti UniFi) provide more reliable, faster Wi-Fi than mesh — multiple APs in a home eliminate dead zones
- **NAS + Docker**: NAS (e.g. Synology, QNAP) = network-attached storage, acts like a personal cloud; can also run Docker containers for self-hosted apps like Plex, Home Assistant, AdGuard
